## 3. Visual Layout & Composition  
**Center-dominant designs** remain key.  Current DTG trends emphasize *“bold, larger-than-life graphics”*. In other words, make the animal character or icon **large and central** (as your system already does) so it reads at thumbnail size.  Avoid clutter: keep backgrounds simple or transparent.  That said, mix in *text-centric layouts* where appropriate.  For example, fundraisers and streetwear often use small *pocket designs* or repeating text/graphics.  A 2026 design guide notes “repeating text, pocket prints, and arched designs” as hot styles.  This means occasional pocket‑logo layouts (tiny mascots or logos over heart) or typography-driven fronts can appeal to more minimalist tastes.  **Positioning:** Text is usually placed **above/below or integrated with** the image (e.g. in a banner or label). Viral memes often use first-person captions as part of the design, so instruct the AI to overlay the witty phrase around the character without obscuring it. In summary: keep the animal as the focal point (ideal for mobile thumbnails), but also test variants with compact logos or stylish text arrangements.  

## 4. AI Prompting Pitfalls & Solutions  
A common trap is inadvertently generating final “mockup” images instead of raw designs.  For Midjourney, **never** include the word *“T-shirt”* in prompts – it leads to complete shirt mockups that are unusable.  Instead use terms like *“clipart”* or *“illustration”* (e.g. “clipart of a sleepy sloth…”). Always add **negative prompts** to strip out unwanted elements.  For example, use `--no mockup`, `--no watermark`, `--no signature`, `--no 3D`, `--no realistic textures`, and `--no glossy`.  Also specify style cues like *“flat 2D vector art”*, *“screenprint style”*, or *“hand-drawn ink”* to avoid the slick “AI gloss” look.  Aspect ratio must be set for vertical chest prints (e.g. `--ar 2:3` or `--ar 19:20`) so the design isn’t squashed.  In short, prompt for *“clean, flat, high-contrast illustration with transparent background”* and explicitly ban mockups or photorealism.  (For DALL·E 3, similarly specify *“transparent background”* and *“vector style”*.)  

## 5. SEO & Platform Search Behavior  
**Redbubble:**  Official guidance confirms that *title*, *tags*, and *description* all feed the search algorithm roughly equally.  Craft titles of ~50 characters that pack in keywords (e.g. style, subject, mood) but **omit any product type** (“T-shirt”, “poster” etc).  For example, instead of “Cat Coffee Mug,” use “Watercolor Cat with Cactus Painting.” Avoid pointless tag stuffing: the system even cleans duplicate or near-duplicate tags. Use singular, broad keywords (e.g. “cat” not both “cat” and “cats”) and add related concepts. Fill out the description to reinforce context.  

**TeePublic:**  Their own tutorial stresses a single, specific **Main Tag** (one word) as *“weighted heavily in search”*.  Supporting tags (6–15 total) should cover style, content and theme.  Do **not** use vague or irrelevant buzzwords, and stop tagging once you exhaust relevant terms. Redundancies hurt: e.g. once you tag “cat,” skip “cats” or “kitten.” Likewise, keep titles reader‑friendly and descriptive (e.g. “Couch Potato” instead of “Lazy Spud”), since titles also influence search.  

**Algorithms & Penalties:** Both platforms warn against keyword stuffing. Tag overuse or irrelevant tags can actually *reduce* visibility. We found no evidence of a special “AI art penalty” update on these sites – the best approach is to follow SEO best practices (unique artwork with accurate metadata). In summary: use concise, natural phrases that real shoppers would type, avoid repeating the same words, and treat your Title/Main Tag as a search-phrase prime mover. This aligns with Redbubble’s advice to make titles unique and multi-keyword rich and TeePublic’s emphasis on a strong single-word main tag.
